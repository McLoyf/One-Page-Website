A little rant about chess

by Bobert Stevenson

You know what drives me crazy about chess? The way people romanticize it like it’s some pure, flawless test of intellect and artistry, when in reality half the time it feels like a minefield of memorization and psychological traps. People talk about “the beauty of the game” and “infinite possibilities,” but let’s be honest: the first 15 moves of any serious game have been dissected, catalogued, and regurgitated a million times over. Openings aren’t creative expression; they’re weaponized history. If you don’t know your Sicilian lines or your Ruy López sidelines, you’re not "exploring ideas," you’re just getting steamrolled by someone who crammed last night. How is that romantic? That’s like calling standardized testing "art".

And then there’s this false notion that chess is purely logical, that if you just calculate hard enough you’ll find the truth of the position. Nonsense. Chess is emotional warfare disguised as geometry. You’re sitting across from someone who’s trying to set a time bomb three moves deep in a variation you thought was safe, waiting for you to blink so they can spring it and make you feel like an idiot. You don’t lose because you weren’t “smart enough,” you lose because you’re human—because you got tired, or nervous, or you thought a knight fork looked scarier than it was. That’s not a perfect system of logic; that’s gladiatorial psychology dressed up in a board game.

And don’t even get me started on the culture around it. Everyone loves to parade out the image of chess as this noble, intellectual pursuit. Sit in a park long enough and you’ll hear some guy explaining how “chess is life,” as if moving a wooden horse in an L-shape somehow encapsulates the struggles of existence. Meanwhile , in the online chess world, it’s just people rage-quitting, spamming emotes, or playing bullet at 2 a.m. until their brains are fried. Half the players are addicted to the dopamine hit of “one more game,” and the other half are pretending like they’re Carlsen’s heir while blundering their queens in five moves.

But here’s the kicker: despite all of this, despite how infuriatingly self-serious and hypocritical and exhausting chess can be… I still can’t quit it. Because every so often, when the fog of calculation clears and you see the board line up in a way that feels like poetry—when a rook slides into the perfect file, when a knight leaps into an outpost like it was destined to be there—it’s intoxicating. Chess is maddening precisely because it keeps dangling that sense of transcendence in front of you. It’s a game that makes you love it even while you’re cursing it. And maybe that’s the real beauty: not in the moves, not in the openings, but in the fact that it keeps pulling us back in , no matter how much we pretend to hate it.
